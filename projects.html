Project Overview

For this assignment, I drafted a professional email to a business contact (for example, a manager, recruiter, or potential partner). The goal was to communicate clearly, respectfully, and concisely while using proper business email format and tone.

My Approach

I treated the email like a real-world interaction:

  • I used a clear subject line that made the purpose obvious.
  • I opened with a polite, specific greeting instead of something generic.
  • I quickly stated why I was reaching out, what I was asking for, and why it mattered.
  • I closed with appreciation, a clear next step, and a professional sign-off.

Reflection: What I Learned

This activity showed me how much tone and structure matter. Even a small email can either show professionalism or create confusion. I learned to:

  • Avoid unnecessary filler and get to the point
  • Make my requests specific and easy to respond to
  • Respect the reader's time while still sounding human and friendly

These are skills I use regularly now when emailing professors, coworkers, and collaborators.

Data Tracking Activity – Business Data Visualization

cat data_project.md

Project Overview

In the Data Tracking activity, I collected, organized, and visualized data related to a business or communication topic. The main goal was to turn raw numbers into a visual story—and then explain what those numbers actually mean.

[Your chart/graph or data visualization would be placed here]

You can embed an image of your chart or an interactive visualization.

My Approach

I focused on:

  • Choosing a chart type that made the data easiest to understand
  • Labeling clearly so the reader doesn't have to guess what they're looking at
  • Writing a short explanation that answered: "So what?"

Instead of just saying, "Here are the numbers," I tried to say, "Here's what we should do or think based on these numbers."

Reflection: What I Learned

This activity helped me practice data storytelling, not just data display. I learned to:

  • Think about my audience first—what they care about and what decisions they might make
  • Highlight the key trend instead of overwhelming the reader with every detail
  • Use visuals as a support tool for communication, not a replacement for it

These are the same skills you need in real projects: tracking metrics, explaining performance, and making recommendations.

My Role

In the team, I helped with:

  • Structuring the overall flow: introduction, problem, analysis, recommendation, conclusion
  • Designing slides that were clean, visual, and not overloaded with text
  • Practicing transitions between speakers so the presentation felt unified
  • Delivering my portion clearly and staying within the time limit

Reflection: What I Learned

This assignment strengthened my team communication and presentation skills. I learned to:

  • Balance individual responsibility with group coordination
  • Give and receive feedback on slide design and speaking style
  • Speak in a way that is professional but still natural and engaging
  • Handle nerves by preparing thoroughly and practicing out loud

These experiences translate directly to real-world projects, where you have to present ideas, persuade stakeholders, and represent your team professionally.
